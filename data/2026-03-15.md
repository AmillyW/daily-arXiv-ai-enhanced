### [1] [Shifted-geodesic approximation for spinning-body gravitational wave fluxes](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.12189)
*Lisa V. Drummond,Scott A. Hughes,Viktor Skoupý,Philip Lynch,Gabriel Andres Piovano*

Main category: gr-qc

TL;DR: Develops a shifted-geodesic approximation for computing GW fluxes from spinning test bodies on bound Kerr orbits. It efficiently incorporates leading-order spin effects into orbital frequencies and flux calculations via perturbed geodesic motion, with minimal waveform dephasing (~$10^{-2}$ radians/year).

  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: Accurate modeling of extreme mass-ratio inspirals (EMRIs) and intermediate-mass-ratio inspirals (IMRIs) for LISA requires efficient computation of gravitational-wave fluxes from spinning bodies on Kerr black hole orbits. Full self-force calculations with spin are computationally expensive, especially for oscillatory spin terms that contribute little to fluxes.

Method: Perturbs orbital frequencies and integrals of motion to include leading spin effects while maintaining geodesic trajectory structure. This 'shifted-geodesic' framework modifies the fundamental frequencies $\Omega_{r,\theta,\phi}$ and constants of motion ($E$, $L_z$, $Q$) via spin corrections, enabling rapid flux evaluation through standard Teukolsky equation or flux formulas.

Result: The approximation works best for low eccentricity, low inclination, and large semi-latus recta orbits. It becomes less reliable near the separatrix. A diagnostic inspiral shows dephasing of only $\sim10^{-2}$ radians over one year, confirming minimal waveform error when incorporating spin effects through this perturbative approach.

Conclusion: The shifted-geodesic method provides a computationally efficient, pragmatically accurate way to include leading spin effects in EMRI/IMRI flux calculations. While not replacing full self-force treatments, it enables rapid parameter-space studies for LISA data analysis where speed and simplicity are prioritized.

Abstract: We present a shifted-geodesic framework for computing gravitational-wave fluxes from spinning test bodies moving on bound orbits of Kerr black holes. The method provides a simple and efficient means of evaluating energy and angular momentum fluxes incorporating the leading effect of the smaller body's spin. Because post-adiabatic corrections, including secondary spin contributions, are subdominant to the leading adiabatic terms, this approximation is well justified. In particular, we find that oscillatory spin terms typically contribute very little to fluxes, but their contribution to the description of orbits is computationally expensive, making such terms a natural target for approximation. In our framework, orbital frequencies and integrals of the motion are perturbed to include spin effects, while the trajectory retains the global structure of geodesic motion. This simplifies the computation of gravitational radiation. The shifted-geodesic approximation is most reliable for orbits with lower eccentricity, lower inclination, and larger semi-latus recta. The approximation becomes less reliable as we approach the separatrix between stable and unstable orbits; fortunately, many inspirals spend less time in this region of parameter space. A diagnostic inspiral evolution shows very small dephasing due to use of the shifted-geodesic approximation ($\approx10^{-2}$ radians over 1 year), confirming that spin-induced flux corrections can be accurately included using this simple modification to a geodesic trajectory. This approximation provides a rapid and convenient way to compute spinning-body orbits, but is not intended to replace more accurate treatments. We propose it as a pragmatic alternative when speed and simplicity are prioritized, enabling efficient EMRI/IMRI flux calculations and supporting parameter-space studies for LISA. (Abridged)
